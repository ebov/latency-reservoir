# Rate heterogeneity in Ebola outbreaks

The large West African and North Kivu outbreaks have seeded a number of seemingly latent infections,
which have gone on to spark secondary transmission chains months and in some cases years after the initial infection.
Genomes isolated from these secondary cases harbour far fewer mutations than expected given the rate of Ebola evolution
during human outbreaks. How the virus persists in a host for such a long period of time with limited mutation and 
presumably little replication is unknown. That it does seems unavoidable.

The rate exploration above suggest something similar may take place in the unobserved reservoir. 
'Latency' defined as a prolonged period of limited replication may be an evolutionary trait of Ebola. 

Here we propose a mechanistic rate heterogeneity model to capture these dynamics.
